Welcome back. Last week we set out the map for the whole course, the idea that technology has a terrain we can learn to read. This week we pick up the instrument we will use to read it for the rest of the term. If week one was orientation, week two is the lens. Everything that comes after, every system we audit, every case we open, we are going to look at through the ideas we build today. So it is worth slowing down here.

Let me start with the sentence that the whole week turns on. A system can be racist with no racist in it. Sit with that, because your instinct is going to fight it. When most people hear the word racism, they picture a person. Someone with a slur, someone with intent, someone you could point to. And that kind of racism is real. But it is not the kind this course is built to study. The move we make this week, and it is the single most important move in the course, is to stop asking whether a system intends harm, and start asking what it actually does, to whom, and who pays for it. Intent is not the test. Outcome is the test.

That shift has a name and a tradition behind it, and that is our first idea, critical race theory. Critical race theory is a body of scholarship with one central claim. Racism is ordinary and structural. It is built into laws, institutions, data, defaults, and everyday habits, not only into individual attitudes. And here is the part that matters for a technology course. A system can produce racially unequal outcomes while looking completely neutral. No rule that names race. No person who means harm. And still, a pattern of who is served and who is failed. So critical race theory trains your attention away from what a system says about itself, and toward what it does when it runs. That is why in this course we study design and data, not only feelings and intentions.

Now let me introduce the scholar at the centre of this week, because she gives us the phrase that will follow us all term. Ruha Benjamin, in her book Race After Technology, names something she calls the New Jim Code. Listen to the phrase. Jim Crow was the era of open, legal racial segregation, a system everyone could see and name. Benjamin's argument is that discrimination did not disappear. It moved. It put on new clothing. Her definition is precise, so let me give it to you carefully. The New Jim Code is the use of new technologies that reflect and reproduce existing inequities, while being promoted and perceived as more objective or progressive than the discriminatory systems of an earlier era. Read that again in your head. The harm is old. The packaging is new. And the packaging is the trick, because a system sold as objective is a system people stop questioning.

Here is why Benjamin matters so much for the shape of this course. She does not leave the New Jim Code as one big idea. She breaks it into four dimensions, four distinct ways that old racism gets carried forward in new technology. I am going to name all four now, and I want you to hear them as a table of contents for the weeks ahead. The first is engineered inequity, harm that is designed into a system. The second is default discrimination, harm that comes from the ordinary settings and defaults, with no rule and no villain. The third is coded exposure, the uneven way systems make some people hyper-visible and others invisible. And the fourth is technological benevolence, the trap where a tool sold as helping actually deepens the harm. Hold onto those four, because they are not just this week's content. Engineered inequity is next week. Default discrimination is the week after. Coded exposure is week five, the facial recognition audit. And technological benevolence returns later in the term. So this week you are being handed the frame, and the coming weeks each take one piece of it and go deep.

Now I want to add the second thinker for this week, because Benjamin's frame has a partner, and the partner tells you where to look. Her name is Kimberle Crenshaw, a legal scholar and one of the founders of critical race theory. Crenshaw first named intersectionality in the late nineteen eighties, and in a landmark essay called Mapping the Margins she works the idea out in full. Here is the idea in plain terms. Systems of identity and power do not act one at a time. Race, gender, class, and other categories overlap, and a person who sits at the intersection of two of them can face a harm that looking at either one alone would completely miss. Crenshaw built this studying violence against women of colour, and her point was that an analysis that looked at race by itself, or gender by itself, kept losing the very people who were hit hardest, because they fell through the gap between the two. Keep that word, gap. It comes back in week five, when Buolamwini and Gebru make intersectionality measurable in a facial-analysis system, and the group that falls through the gap turns out to be darker-skinned women. Crenshaw is the reason we know to test at the intersection, and not one axis at a time.

Our third reading this week widens the picture from theory to a system you touch every day. Safiya Umoja Noble, in Algorithms of Oppression, looked at something as ordinary as a search engine. Search engines present themselves as neutral. You type a question, the machine returns the world's information, ranked by relevance. That is the story they tell. Noble showed that when she searched for Black women and girls, the results she got back were demeaning and stereotyped, shaped by what sold and what got clicked, not by any honest measure of who those girls were. And her point is not that one search was a glitch that got fixed. Her point is structural. The racism sat in the design, in how the system ranked and surfaced and profited from information. Noble pairs with Benjamin perfectly. Benjamin gives you the frame, old harm in new packaging. Noble shows you the frame at work in a tool you would have sworn was neutral.

So let me pull the week together, because these three readings are really one argument. Critical race theory tells you that racism is structural, living in systems and not only in hearts. Benjamin names the technological version of that, the New Jim Code, and breaks it into the four dimensions that organize our term. Crenshaw tells you where the harm concentrates and where to look for it, at the intersection. And Noble shows you the whole thing running inside an everyday product that claimed to be neutral. Underneath all of it is that one analytic shift, the one I asked you to sit with at the start. Not, did this system intend harm. But, what does this system do, to whom, and who pays.

That question is the instrument. For the rest of the term, every time we open a case, we are going to hold it up and ask it. And you will notice something as we go. The systems will keep insisting they are neutral. Objective. Data-driven. Progressive. And you will have learned this week to hear that word, neutral, not as the end of the conversation, but as the beginning of it. Neutral for whom. Measured how. Who was in the data, and who was left out.

If this week feels dense, that is by design. You are not just learning facts here, you are picking up a way of seeing that the rest of the course will sharpen. So do the readings knowing what each one is for. Benjamin for the frame and the four dimensions. Crenshaw for the intersection. Noble for the frame made real in an everyday system. And next week, we take the first of Benjamin's four dimensions, engineered inequity, and we watch how harm gets built into a system on purpose. I will see you there.
