Week 3: Engineered Inequity

BFS218, Racism and the Digital Age

Welcome to week three. Last week we picked up the lens, critical race theory, the idea that racism is structural, and Ruha Benjamin's name for its technological form, the New Jim Code. We ended by laying out her four dimensions, four ways that old racism gets carried forward in new technology, and I promised we would take them one at a time. This is the week we start. Today we go inside the first of the four dimensions, and its name is engineered inequity.

Let me open with a question, because Benjamin opens with it too, and it is the question this whole week turns on. Are robots racist. Say it out loud and notice what your mind does with it. It sounds almost like a joke, because a robot has no feelings. It cannot hate anyone. It does not wake up in the morning carrying a grudge. So the easy answer is no, of course not, a machine is just a machine. Benjamin's answer is where the week begins. Yes, she says, a robot can be racist. Not because it holds hatred inside it, but because it was designed inside a society that is already structured by racism, and it carries that structure forward. Hear what that does. It takes the whole question of racism and moves it off of feelings, off of intent, and onto design. The machine does not need to mean anything. It only needs to be built by us, inside the world as it already is.

So here is the term for the week, and I want you to hold both words. Engineered inequity. Engineered inequity is Benjamin's first dimension of the New Jim Code, and she defines it as technology that, by its design, amplifies social hierarchies based on race, class, and gender. Let me slow down on each half of that, because both halves are carrying weight. First, the word engineered. To engineer something is to build it on purpose, through a chain of choices. Nothing about a system is an accident of nature. Someone chose the data it learns from. Someone chose what the system is trying to predict. Someone chose what counts as a good outcome, and what counts as success. Every one of those is a decision, and every decision is a place where the inequality already sitting in the world can get built into the machine. That is what engineered means here. The harm did not fall from the sky. It was assembled.

Now the second half, and this is the key word, so I am going to say it slowly. Amplify. Engineered inequity amplifies a hierarchy. It does not, on its own, create one. That distinction is the heart of the week, so let me make it plain. The gap was already there. The racism was already in the housing, in the hiring, in the policing, in the history. What the technology does is take that existing gap and scale it. It makes it faster, wider, cheaper to reproduce, and much harder to see. Think of an amplifier behind a guitar. It does not write the note. It takes a note that is already being played and makes it fill the whole room. Engineered inequity is an amplifier pointed at an inequality that was already sounding.

Let me make that concrete with an example we already met in week one. A landlord who refuses a tenant is an old, familiar harm, one person doing something to another person. Now replace the landlord with a tenant-screening algorithm, a system that scores applicants and tells a building who to let in. That system did not invent housing discrimination. It inherited it. It learned from a world where certain neighbourhoods, certain names, certain histories were already treated as risks. And then it did the thing machines do best. It scaled. One biased landlord can turn away the people standing in front of him. One biased screening system can turn away thousands of people at once, in a fraction of a second, in cities its designer will never visit. Same harm, engineered and amplified, wearing the calm face of a number on a screen.

And that calm face is the other half of what makes engineered inequity dangerous. Benjamin's definition says the harm presents itself as neutral or efficient, even while it widens a gap that already exists. This is the signature of the New Jim Code, the thing you learned to look for last week. The tenant-screening system is not advertised as a way to keep certain people out. It is advertised as efficiency. As objectivity. As finally taking messy human bias out of the decision. So the very feature that makes it harmful, that it runs at scale with no argument and no appeal, is sold to you as the feature that makes it fair. The inequity hides inside the word efficient.

Now let me connect this back to the shift we made last week, because engineered inequity is that shift made specific. In week two we said, stop asking whether a system intends harm, and start asking what it does, to whom, and who pays. Engineered inequity takes that one step further and asks, how was it built. Because if the harm is engineered, then it was designed, and if it was designed, then somebody made choices, and choices can be examined. Choices can be named. Choices can be made differently. That is actually the hopeful edge hiding inside a hard idea. A harm that was engineered is a harm that was authored, and anything that was authored can be audited.

So here is where the auditor's question comes in, the one we carry all term. When you meet a system this week, you do not ask, is it neutral, or is it accurate. You ask, neutral for whom. Accurate for whom. Who pays when it is wrong. And, because this week is about design, one more. Who decided that was good enough. Somebody signed off on that tenant-screening system. Somebody looked at how it performed and said, ship it. Engineered inequity tells you that person, and that decision, are part of the machine now. The values did not disappear when the system was automated. They got built in, and then they got hidden.

Let me set the frame for where we are, so you can see the shape of the next few weeks. Engineered inequity, this week, is the most deliberate corner of the New Jim Code. It is harm that got designed in, built by choices, amplified by scale. But Benjamin's larger argument is that harm does not always need that much authorship. Sometimes it does not take a bold design choice at all. Sometimes it only takes a default, a setting left the way it has always been, one group's world quietly treated as the norm. And that is exactly where we go next week. Week four is default discrimination, the second dimension, harm that arrives with no rule and no villain, riding in on the ordinary settings of a system. If this week showed you inequity that was engineered on purpose, next week shows you inequity that needs no purpose at all.

So going into your work this week, hold the robot question close, because it is the whole lesson in five words. Are robots racist. Not because they feel anything. Because they are built inside our world, by our choices, and they carry our hierarchies forward while calling it efficiency. Watch for the word engineered, and watch for the word amplify. The gap was already there. The technology made it bigger, faster, and harder to see. Next week, we take away even the design choice, and ask how a system can discriminate with nobody deciding anything at all. I will see you in week four.
