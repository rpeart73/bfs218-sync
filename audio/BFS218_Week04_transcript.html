Welcome back to week four. Last week we opened the first of Ruha Benjamin's four dimensions of the New Jim Code, engineered inequity, and we watched harm get built into a system on purpose, by design. This week we take the second dimension, and it is a stranger one. Because last week there was at least a design choice to point at. Someone decided. This week, very often, no one decided anything at all, and the harm still arrives.

The title for this week is default discrimination. Sit with that first word, default, because the whole dimension lives inside it. But let me start with a different, smaller word, one I want you to carry all week, because it is the word people reach for to make a harm disappear. The word is glitch. When a system fails a person, when it misreads them, or locks them out, or treats them as if they are not quite there, we reach for that word almost automatically. It was a glitch. A bug. A one-off. Something that slipped through. This week is about what happens when you stop trusting that word, and start asking a harder question. Is the glitch systemic. That is Benjamin's framing question for this entire dimension, and by the end of today I want it to be the first thing you think when someone waves a harm away as a glitch.

So let me define the term plainly, the way I want it to sit in your notes. Default discrimination is harm that arrives through the defaults of a system. The settings, the data, and the assumptions that quietly treat one group's world as the norm. And here is the part that makes it different from last week. It does not require a racist designer. It does not require a rule that names race, or a person who means any harm at all. It requires only one thing. That the inequity already out in the world gets left, unexamined, sitting in the defaults. So hold the two dimensions side by side. Engineered inequity was harm that someone built in. Default discrimination is harm that no one took out.

Let me make sure we all mean the same thing by a default, because it is one of those words we use without ever looking at it. A default is the setting that is already chosen. The option that is ticked before you arrive. The way a system behaves when nobody makes an active choice. Think of the phone in your pocket. It shipped with dozens of settings already selected, and you have changed, what, three of them. The rest you accepted, not because you decided they were right, but because they were simply already there. That is the quiet power of a default. Most people never touch it. A default is a decision that gets made once, by someone you will never meet, and then it applies silently to everyone who comes after. Which means a default is never really neutral. It always carries an assumption inside it, an assumption about who the normal user is. Whose name fits the form. Whose face the camera expects. Whose voice the system was tuned to hear. And if that assumed normal user was drawn from a world that already runs on inequity, then the inequity rides straight in, for free, wearing the plain clothes of a setting that nobody thought to question.

There is a piece of engineering language I want to hand you this week, because once you hear it you will not be able to unhear it. When designers build to a default user, everyone outside that default gets a name. They get called edge cases. An edge case is exactly what it sounds like. A case out at the edge. Rare, unusual, not the main thing, not worth the extra work to design for. Now hear what that word does when the default user was one skin tone, one kind of name, one way of speaking, and the edge cases are everyone else. A whole community can get quietly filed under edge case. Not out of hatred. Out of a spreadsheet. Out of a decision that they were not the assumed majority, so the system did not have to work as well for them. And when it then fails them, the failure gets called, you already know the word, a glitch.

So let us go back to Benjamin's question and take it seriously. Is the glitch systemic. Her argument in Race After Technology, and it is the beating heart of this dimension, is that we have the word glitch backwards. We treat a glitch as noise, as the system failing to do its job. But when the same kind of person keeps getting misread, keeps getting locked out, keeps having to work around a system that was never built with them in mind, that is not noise. That is signal. The glitch is the system doing exactly what its defaults set it up to do. So say it plainly. A glitch that always lands on the same people is not a glitch. It is a pattern wearing a glitch's clothing. And the move of this week is to stop accepting the word at face value, and start asking whether the accident is really an accident, or the design quietly showing through.

I want to connect this back to the lens we built in week two, because default discrimination is almost the purest proof of the point we made there. Remember the shift. We stopped asking whether a system intends harm, and we started asking what it does, to whom, and who pays. Engineered inequity could still leave you a little room to argue about intent, because at least someone made a design choice. But default discrimination closes that door completely. Here there may be no intent anywhere in the room. No rule. No villain. No one who wished anyone any harm at all. And the racial harm happens anyway, because the world the defaults came from was already unequal, and nobody did the work to correct for it. If you ever needed to prove that intent is not the test, this dimension is your proof. A system with a perfectly clean conscience can still produce a harmful outcome.

So what does an auditor do with a default. The same thing we always do in this course. We refuse to let the system's own story be the last word. When a system says, this is just the standard setting, this is just how it works out of the box, we ask the question that runs through the whole term. Not, is this setting neutral. But, neutral for whom. Who was the standard user this default was built around. Who becomes the edge case. Who pays when the default does not fit them. And who decided that setting was good enough to ship. Because a default is a decision. Somebody chose it, or somebody chose not to look at it, and either way there is a person who can be asked, why this one, and why did you stop there.

So here is where we have travelled. Week three, engineered inequity, harm built into a system on purpose. Week four, default discrimination, harm that rides in on the ordinary settings, with no rule and no villain, and then gets excused as a glitch. Two dimensions down. Two different ways the same old harm gets carried forward in new technology. One by design. One by default. Next week we take the third dimension, coded exposure, and we make all of this measurable. We put a real audit into your hands. We look at facial analysis and at speech recognition, at who a system is built to see and hear clearly and who it misses, and we watch the gap open up with actual numbers. Everything we said today about defaults, about a normal user the system was quietly tuned to, is about to stop being abstract. Next week you get to measure it. Keep the word glitch close, and keep it suspicious. I will see you in week five.
