Let me introduce the two researchers at the centre of this week. Joy Buolamwini was a graduate student working with facial analysis software, and she noticed something strange. The system would not reliably detect her face, a dark-skinned Black woman, until she put on a white mask. Sit with that image for a second. Instead she treated it as a measurement problem. She and Timnit Gebru decided to test the claim of accuracy properly.

Here is how they did it, because the method is the lesson. The face collections these systems had usually been tested on leaned heavily toward lighter skin and toward men, so a system could look accurate overall while never really being checked on everyone. So Buolamwini and Gebru built a new benchmark on purpose, balanced by gender and balanced by skin type, so that no group could hide inside an average. Then they ran three commercial gender classification systems against it, the kind of systems that were already being sold to real customers. IBM. Microsoft. And a company called Face plus plus. I want to name why the single-axis tests missed this, because that idea is bigger than facial recognition. It comes from the legal scholar Kimberle Crenshaw, and the word is intersectionality. Crenshaw's point, first made about discrimination law, is that some harms only become visible at the intersection of two categories. If you only ask "how does the system do on women," you fold darker-skinned women in with lighter-skinned women, and the average looks fine. If you only ask "how does it do on darker skin," you fold darker-skinned women in with darker-skinned men, and again the average softens the blow. The harm lives exactly where the two axes cross, and a test that looks at one axis at a time is almost designed to miss it. So intersectionality is not a slogan here. In this week it is a testing instruction. It tells you where to look.

Buolamwini gave the whole phenomenon a name of her own, and it is the second term I want you to leave with. The coded gaze. You have heard of the male gaze, or the way a camera can be built around who is assumed to be watching. The coded gaze is the same idea moved into software. It names whose face a system is built to see, and tested to see, and therefore actually sees. A system trained mostly on lighter-skinned faces learns lighter-skinned faces best. Then it is sold as if it sees everyone equally. The gaze is coded into it, quietly, through the data it was fed and the faces its makers chose to check it against.

Now, this is the point in the week where a student sometimes says, all right, but nobody wrote a racist rule. Nobody sat down and typed "fail the darker-skinned women." That objection is correct, and it is exactly the trap. The scholar Ruha Benjamin has a name for this, and it is our third term. The New Jim Code. Her argument is that discrimination today often does not need a bigot with a rule. It gets produced by systems that are presented as neutral, even as they reproduce old hierarchies. Look at what happened here. No rule was written. The gap was measured, publicly, with clear numbers. And the products still shipped, and were still sold as neutral tools. The harm is in whose faces the system was built and tested to see. The neutrality is the packaging.

Benjamin also gives us the phrase in our title, coded exposure, and it sharpens the whole week. Her point is that visibility itself is designed unevenly. Some people are made hyper-visible to systems. They are watched, scanned, tracked, flagged. Other people can move through the same spaces barely registered at all. So the problem is not simply that a face system is less accurate on some faces. It is that being seen and being unseen are both distributed, and they are distributed along the same old lines. Being misread by a camera is one harm. Being the group a surveillance system is tuned to watch is another. Coded exposure holds both of those at once.

Let me stop you from filing this under "a face thing," because your second reading this week refuses to let you. Koenecke and colleagues, in twenty twenty, ran the same kind of careful audit, but on the ear instead of the eye. They tested five leading automated speech recognition systems, the engines behind voice assistants and automatic captions, from Amazon, Apple, Google, IBM, and Microsoft. They fed them matched recordings of Black and white speakers. The systems misrecognized Black speakers about twice as often. The average word error rate was about zero point three five for Black speakers against about zero point one nine for white speakers. Twice as likely to be misheard. And here is the detail that closes the door on the easy explanations. The gap held even when Black and white speakers said the exact same words. The authors trace it back to acoustic models trained on too little Black speech. Same story as the faces. The system learned best what it was shown most.

So say it plainly. The coded gaze fails by ear as well as by eye. Whether a system is reading your face or transcribing your voice, the question underneath is the same. Whose data taught it. Whose accuracy was checked. Who was in the room, and who was assumed.

Now let me make sure this does not stay abstract, because the reason we care is the deployment. These are not lab curiosities. Facial analysis gets wired into policing, into airports, into building access, into the phone in your pocket. Speech recognition gets wired into captions a Deaf student relies on, into a hiring tool that scores a recorded interview, into the assistant that is supposed to understand a command the first time. Now be precise about what that thirty four percent measured. It was a gender-classification gap, the system guessing male or female, not a police identification rate. But hold onto why it still matters. The same kind of facial-analysis and recognition technology, built and checked the same careless way, gets wired into higher-stakes systems, including policing, where being misread is not a rounding error but a person pulled aside. Those systems need their own testing, on their own task, and Gender Shades is the warning about what happens when they do not get it. A caption garbles a lecture for the student who needs it most. A voice interface works for one accent and fights another. The average said the product was fine. The intersection said who would pay for that.

Which brings us to what you actually do this week, and it is a skill, not a mood. You become the auditor. The move you are practising has three steps, and you saw all three in Gender Shades. First, refuse the single headline number. Second, disaggregate, split the data by group, and then split it again where the groups cross. Third, ask the intersectional question out loud. Who carries the error that the average hid. That is a transferable habit. You can point it at a face system, a speech system, a hiring score, a risk score, a fraud flag. Any time someone hands you one clean number about a system that touches people, the professional response is not to admire it. It is to ask the number to come apart.
