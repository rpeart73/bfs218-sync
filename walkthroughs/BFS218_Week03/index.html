What is engineered inequity?

Benjamin's claim is precise: engineered inequity is technology that, by its design, explicitly amplifies social hierarchies based on race, class, and gender. The harm is built into the design. The system presents itself as neutral or efficient while widening a gap that already exists. That is the definition to carry all week.

Insight

The harm is in the design, not in a feeling or a mistake. The system can look neutral and efficient and still widen the gap.

The key move: amplify, not create

The careful move this week is the word amplify. Engineered inequity does not invent inequality from nothing. It takes an existing gap, an inequality already in society, and a technology designed without correcting for it, and the result is a wider gap, made faster and harder to escape. Because the problem is the design, the fix is never just better intentions, it is a different design.

Insight

Amplify is the whole argument. The inequality was there first; the system makes it wider, faster, and harder to escape, so the fix is different design, not better feelings.

Think about it

What is the difference between a technology that creates a bias and one that amplifies an inequality that already exists? Why does Benjamin insist on the second?

Are robots racist?

This is Benjamin's framing question for engineered inequity. Her answer: robots can be racist, not because a machine feels hatred, but because it is designed inside a society already structured by racism, and it carries that structure forward. The question moves us, again, from intent to design.

Insight

Robots can be racist, no hatred required. A machine built inside an unequal society carries that structure forward unless it is designed against it.

Why design, not intent

Here is the mechanism. Start with an unequal society. Add a design that ignores that inequality and treats the unequal world as normal. The system inherits the inequality and forwards it, no hatred required. That chain, unequal world plus a design that does not correct for it, equals amplified harm, is what makes engineered inequity structural rather than personal.

Insight

An unequal society plus a design that treats it as normal equals amplified harm. No villain is needed for the harm to be real.

How to spot engineered inequity

Ask three questions of any system you study this week. First, does it widen a gap that was already there, rather than create one from nothing? Second, what specific design choice, in the data, the defaults, or who it was built for, produces that widening? Third, who benefits from the design, and who carries the cost when it is wrong? These three questions are your tool for the rest of the dimension.

Insight

Three questions: which existing gap, which design choice widens it, and who carries the cost. That is how you turn the definition into something you can actually apply.

Engineered inequity is intersectional

Benjamin frames engineered inequity across race, class, and gender at once. On the same person, the amplifications can stack. Race is one axis the design amplifies, class is another, gender is another. Where those axes meet, the harm is heaviest, because a single system can widen several gaps in the same person at the same time.

Insight

Race, class, and gender are not separate problems here. The harm lands heaviest where the axes meet on one person.
